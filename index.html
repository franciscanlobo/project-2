Learn More About Kyoto

Kyoto City has a living culture that has been handed down for over a thousand years. You can feel it in how the streets, kimono, shrines, and temples make you aware of the changing of the seasons.

It is our responsibility as people living today to pass on the city that has nurtured this irreplaceable culture, so that it will flourish another thousand years.

This is a culture we want to cultivate together. It is not only for those who reside and work here, but also for those who visit Kyoto to experience the traditions that live on in this city.

What's special about Kyoto?

What makes Kyoto such a well-loved and highly regarded city?
Here are a few points that can help in answering that question.

Japan’s Former Imperial Capital

京都の古都としての歴史

For over a thousand years, Kyoto served as Japan’s political and cultural heart. Its palaces, shrines, and gardens reflect centuries of refined craftsmanship and court traditions, preserving the elegance of Japan’s classical era.

Home to Iconic UNESCO Heritage Sites

世界遺産のまち

Kyoto holds some of Japan’s most significant temples and shrines, including Kiyomizudera, Kinkakuji, and Fushimi Inari. These sacred landmarks blend architecture, spirituality, and nature into immersive, contemplative experiences.

Living Tradition & Handcrafted Culture

伝統工芸と文化芸能

Kyoto is known for artisanal crafts such as silk weaving, tea ceramics, lacquerware, and kimono dyeing. These skills are passed down through generations, keeping traditional Japanese artistry alive in a modern world.

Birthplace of Manga & Cultural Storytelling

漫画文化の源流

Kyoto’s Kozanji Temple preserves the Chōjū-jinbutsu-giga, one of Japan’s earliest manga-style illustrated scrolls. This legacy continues today, influencing the country’s storytelling, animation, and pop culture identity.

Refined Culinary Heritage

京料理と食文化

Kyoto’s cuisine highlights subtle flavors and seasonality. From delicate kaiseki mea pb-4ls to matcha from Uji, the region’s food culture emphasizes balance, artistry, and the beauty of simple, thoughtful preparation.

Harmony Between Old and Modern Life

人々が集まるまち

Kyoto remains a living city — not a museum. Wooden machiya homes, teahouses, gardens, and festivals coexist with contemporary shops, universities, and local neighborhoods, creating a quiet balance of past and present.

Kyoto Through the Seasons

Kyoto is a city that transforms with the seasons. Its temples, gardens, and streets reflect the subtle rhythms of nature — inviting visitors to return and rediscover the city again and again.

Fushimi Inari Taisha
伏見稲荷大社

Fushimi Inari Taisha is Kyoto’s most iconic Shinto shrine, dedicated to Inari Ōkami—the deity of rice, prosperity, and good fortune. Founded in 711 A.D., it’s famous for its thousands of vermilion torii gates (Senbon Torii) that lead up sacred Mount Inari.

Highlights
  • Senbon Torii: Endless red gates donated for blessings.
  • Fox Statues (Kitsune): Messengers of Inari, symbolizing protection and wisdom.
  • Mount Inari Trail: 4 km scenic hike with smaller shrines and Kyoto views.
Visitor Info
  • Open: 24 hours, free admission
  • Best Time: Early morning or evening
  • Duration: 2–3 hours
  • Tip: Wear comfy shoes and be respectful at shrines
Getting There

From Kyoto Station, take the JR Nara Line to Inari Station (5 minutes). The shrine entrance is right across the station.

Arashiyama Bamboo Grove
嵐山竹林の小径

The Arashiyama Bamboo Grove offers a peaceful walk through towering green bamboo stalks. The light and wind through the bamboo create one of Kyoto’s most serene experiences.

Highlights
  • Bamboo Pathway: A 500-meter scenic trail surrounded by dense bamboo.
  • Tenryu-ji Temple: UNESCO World Heritage site adjacent to the grove.
  • Togetsukyo Bridge: Famous nearby landmark offering views of Arashiyama’s landscape.
Visitor Info
  • Open: 24 hours, free admission
  • Best Time: Early morning for fewer visitors
  • Duration: Around 1 hour
  • Tip: Visit at sunrise for soft lighting and quieter paths
Getting There

From Kyoto Station, take the JR Sagano Line to Saga-Arashiyama Station (15 minutes), then walk 10 minutes to the grove.

Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion)
金閣寺

The Golden Pavilion is one of Kyoto’s most stunning landmarks. Its top floors are covered in gold leaf and reflected beautifully in the surrounding pond, symbolizing harmony between heaven and earth.

Highlights
  • Golden Pavilion: A three-story structure symbolizing harmony between heaven and earth.
  • Kyoko-chi Pond: Mirror-like water reflecting the pavilion and nearby pines.
  • Tea Garden: Small teahouse serving traditional matcha and sweets.
Visitor Info
  • Open: 9:00 AM – 5:00 PM
  • Admission: ¥500
  • Best Time: Autumn and winter for vivid reflections
  • Duration: Around 1 hour
Getting There

From Kyoto Station, take a City Bus 101 or 205 to Kinkaku-ji Michi Stop (about 40 minutes), then walk 5 minutes to the temple.
